Overview

This chapter introduces probability as a mathematical framework for describing uncertainty in astronomical observations. Even when physical laws are deterministic, limited measurements and noise make outcomes uncertain.

Uncertainty in Observational Science

The textbook emphasizes that probability theory models uncertainty. Astronomers cannot observe all properties of a system perfectly, and measurements are affected by instrumental limitations and noise.

As a result, probability is used to quantify how uncertain our knowledge is, rather than assuming measurements are exact.

Experiments, Outcome Spaces, and Events

Experiments

An experiment is any process whose outcome cannot be predicted with certainty before it occurs. Examples include counting photons detected by a telescope or measuring a star’s radial velocity.

Outcome Space

The outcome space is the set of all possible outcomes of an experiment. For example, photon counting experiments may have outcomes {0, 1, 2, 3, …}.

Events

An event is a subset of the outcome space, such as measuring a value above a certain threshold.

Axioms of Probability

The book introduces three basic axioms:

  • Probabilities are non-negative.
  • The probability of the entire outcome space is 1.
  • For mutually exclusive events, probabilities add.

Conditional Probability

Conditional probability describes how probabilities change when new information becomes available. It is widely used in astronomy when observations depend on selection effects or incomplete data.

Bayes’ Theorem

Bayes’ theorem provides a method for updating beliefs using observed data. It connects prior knowledge, the likelihood of the data, and the updated posterior belief.

Random Variables

A random variable assigns numerical values to outcomes. Discrete random variables describe countable outcomes, while continuous random variables describe measurements over a range of values.

Probability Distributions

Probability distributions describe how likely different values of a random variable are. The cumulative distribution function gives the probability that a variable is less than or equal to a given value.

Limit Theorems

The Law of Large Numbers explains why averages stabilize as more data are collected. The Central Limit Theorem explains why normal distributions appear so frequently in astronomical data analysis.
