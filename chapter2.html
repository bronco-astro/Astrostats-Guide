Chapter 2 — Probability Concepts

Probability is used to describe uncertainty in astronomy and physics. Most measurements are not exact. Instruments have limits, observations are incomplete, and noise is always present. Because of this, probability is used to describe what is known and how uncertain that knowledge is.

Even when a physical process follows strict laws, the outcome is treated as uncertain if all controlling factors cannot be measured. For example, photon counts from a telescope follow physical laws, but the exact number detected during an observation cannot be predicted ahead of time.

Uncertainty and observations

Astronomical data always contain uncertainty. This uncertainty can come from measurement errors, limited observing time, or physical processes that cannot be fully observed.

Probability does not describe randomness in nature itself. It describes uncertainty in human knowledge. A situation may be deterministic in principle, but uncertain in practice.

Probability rules

Probability values follow a few basic rules. Probability values are never negative. The total probability over all possible outcomes equals one. When outcomes cannot occur together, their probabilities add.

These rules allow probability to work as a consistent mathematical system.

Conditional probability

Probability often changes when new information is known. Conditional probability describes how likelihood changes once additional information is available.

In astronomy, observations are often affected by detection limits and selection effects. Conditional probability is used to account for these conditions.

Bayes’ theorem

Bayes’ theorem connects prior knowledge with new data. It provides a rule for updating beliefs after measurements are made.

The idea is to start with an initial expectation, compare it with observed data, and update the expectation using the data.

Random variables and measurements

Measurements are represented using random variables. A random variable assigns numerical values to uncertain quantities such as brightness, distance, velocity, or photon counts.

Some variables take only whole-number values, while others vary continuously.

Probability distributions

Probability distributions describe how likely different values of a measurement are. A distribution can be described using a cumulative distribution function or a density function.

For continuous measurements, probabilities are assigned to ranges rather than exact values.

Independence and assumptions

Many statistical methods assume measurements do not influence each other and come from the same underlying distribution. These assumptions simplify analysis but are not always valid in astronomy.

Correlated data and time-dependent measurements often violate these assumptions.

Large-sample behavior

When many measurements are collected, regular patterns appear. Averages tend to stabilize as more data are added.

Sums of many independent measurements often follow a normal distribution, even when the original data do not.
