Chapter 12 — Spatial Point Processes

This chapter is about data that describe locations in space. Instead of measuring values at fixed points, the data consist of positions of objects.

In astronomy, this usually means positions of stars, galaxies, clusters, or detected events on the sky. The goal is to understand spatial patterns rather than individual measurements.

What makes spatial data different

Spatial data describe where objects occur. The positions are often not independent. Objects may cluster together or avoid each other.

Methods that assume independence cannot describe this behavior. Spatial point processes provide tools to analyze these patterns.

Point patterns

A point pattern is a set of locations in space. Each point represents one object. The pattern may appear random, clustered, or regularly spaced.

Plotting the points is always the first step, but visual inspection alone is not enough.

Complete spatial randomness

Complete spatial randomness is a reference idea. Under this assumption, points occur independently and uniformly over space.

This model is often used as a baseline for comparison.

Poisson point process

The Poisson point process is the simplest spatial point process. Points occur independently, and the number of points in a region follows a Poisson distribution.

The average density is constant over space. This process is commonly used as a null model.

Intensity function

The intensity function describes how point density varies over space. It gives the expected number of points per unit area.

In some cases, intensity is constant. In other cases, it changes with position.

Inhomogeneous Poisson processes

Many astronomical datasets are not uniform. Inhomogeneous Poisson processes allow intensity to vary across space.

This variation may come from physical structure or observational effects.

Clustering and interaction

Not all spatial patterns can be explained by intensity alone. Some point processes include interaction between points.

Points may attract each other or repel each other, leading to clustering or regular spacing.

Ripley’s K function

Ripley’s K function is a common tool for studying spatial clustering. It measures how many points lie within a given distance of a typical point.

Deviations from random expectations indicate clustering or inhibition.

Pair correlation function

The pair correlation function describes how point density changes with distance. It provides a local view of clustering.

This method helps identify characteristic spatial scales.

Edge effects

Spatial analysis is affected by boundaries of the observation region. Points near edges have fewer neighbors within the observed area.

Edge correction methods are needed to avoid bias.

Marked point processes

Sometimes each point has additional information, called a mark. Marks may represent brightness, mass, or other properties.

Marked point processes study how marks relate to spatial structure.

Applications in astronomy

Spatial point processes are used to study galaxy clustering, star-forming regions, and distributions of detected events.

These methods help separate random structure from physical patterns.

Limits of spatial methods

Results depend on assumptions about the observation region and the underlying process.

Incomplete coverage and selection effects can bias results. Interpretation requires care and physical understanding.
