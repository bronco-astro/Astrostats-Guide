Chapter 4 — Probability Distributions

Probability distributions describe how values of a measurement are distributed. Instead of focusing on a single observation, distributions describe what happens when the same measurement is repeated many times.

In astronomy, distributions are used to model photon counts, measurement errors, detection results, etc.

A distribution is a model. It does not describe exactly how the reality is, but it captures the main behaviour of the data pattern.

The binomial distribution

The binomial distribution is used when a measurement is repeated a fixed number of times, and each repetition has only two possible outcomes. Use coin flip as an example, or Ture/False for the outcomes.

The probability of observing exactly \( k \) successes is

\[ P(k) = \binom{n}{k} p^k (1 - p)^{n - k} \]

where

\[ \binom{n}{k} = \frac{n!}{k!(n-k)!} \]

The Poisson distribution

The Poisson distribution describes how many times something happens within a fixed interval. And λ here is quite important as it characterize the distribution.

The probability of observing \( k \) occurrences is

\[ P(k) = \frac{\lambda^k e^{-\lambda}}{k!} \]

This distribution is often used to model number of seeds in Satsuma grown at Prof.Hartigan's backyard.

The normal distribution

The normal distribution, also called the Gaussian distribution, is often what natural phenomenon spans.

The probability density function is

\[ f(x) = \frac{1}{\sigma \sqrt{2\pi}} \exp\!\left( -\frac{(x - \mu)^2}{2\sigma^2} \right) \]

The normal distribution is symmetric around its center.

Comparing the distributions

The binomial distribution is used when the number of trials is fixed and outcomes are limited to two possibilities.

The Poisson distribution is used when counting how many times something occurs in a fixed interval.

The normal distribution is used for continuous measurements.
